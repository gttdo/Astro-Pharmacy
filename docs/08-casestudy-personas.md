**WHO I DESIGNED FOR**

# The same order is a completely different problem from each side of the bench

Astro's whole job happens between two people who touch the same order under opposite pressures: the **technician** who prepares it, and the **pharmacist** who is accountable for releasing it. I framed their needs as jobs to be done — so the team stayed anchored on outcomes, not features, and so every screen had to answer to a real moment instead of a checklist.

### The Technician — executes under pressure
*Frontline · high-volume shifts*

> "When an order lands in my queue on a busy shift, I want to pick it up, prepare it, and prove each step without breaking my rhythm — so nothing gets lost, and nothing comes back to me as a reject."

**Goals**
- Start a dose in seconds, and always know what's most urgent
- Capture proof *as I work* — not as paperwork afterward
- Never lose a step or a ticket across a chaotic shift

**Frustrations**
- Interrupted constantly; pulled between the hood and the phone
- Re-work when a pharmacist rejects something — without a reason I can see
- Tracking status on a whiteboard that's stale the moment I turn around

*Evidence: 92.8% of hospitals report a shortage of sterile-compounding-experienced technicians, and pharmacy staff are interrupted as often as once every two minutes — the exact conditions under which steps get skipped.*

### The Pharmacist (RPh) — accountable for the release
*Oversight · the one who answers for it*

> "When a preparation reaches me, I want to see exactly what was done — with evidence — without re-gowning or re-doing the work, so I can release it quickly, or catch the problem before it reaches a patient."

**Goals**
- Verify on complete, trustworthy evidence — not a verbal walkthrough
- See ownership and status across the whole queue at a glance
- Hold a defensible record if a dose is ever questioned

**Frustrations**
- "Verifying from memory" — the weakest possible safeguard
- Gowning in and out of the cleanroom just to check a single step
- No way to see where an order is stuck, or whose hands it's in

*Evidence: independent double-checks show no reliable reduction in error (BMJ Quality & Safety, 2020) — verification has to rest on evidence, not a second pair of tired eyes.*

### And the stakeholder who signs the check
Behind both is **pharmacy leadership** — the director who answers for throughput, compliance, and the audit trail. They don't live in the app, but they decide whether it lives. Their job to be done: *"prove the operation is safe, fast, and accountable — to an auditor, a CMO, and a budget."* Designing for them meant the accountability Astro produces had to be a **byproduct of the work**, not a reporting chore bolted on top.

## PRODUCTION NOTES

- **Layout:** two persona cards side by side (Technician / Pharmacist), mirroring the published JTBD card style; leadership as a slim full-width strip beneath. The two cards should feel like *opposite* pressures — speed vs. assurance.
- **The dyad is the point:** visually emphasize that they share one object (the order) from two sides. A small connective motif (the same ticket icon on both cards) reinforces it.
- **Evidence treatment:** keep the italic evidence line per persona — it quietly proves the personas are research-grounded, not invented. Attributed, so it survives interview scrutiny.
- **Voice:** first person only in the framing paragraph ("I framed their needs…"); the personas speak in *their* first person via the JTBD quote.
- **Continuity:** "nothing comes back to me as a reject" (Technician) and "catch the problem before it reaches a patient" (Pharmacist) both point forward to the verification/approve-reject section — the heart of the product.
